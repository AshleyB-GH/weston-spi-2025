# Risk Assessment (RA) & Mitigation

## Introduction

The management of risk is a fundamental part of Dublin Weston Airport's
(DWA's) Safety Management System (SMS).

Risk assessments are a key tool used to identify, evaluate, and reduce
risks to As Low as Reasonably Practicable (ALARP) and the findings of
any risk assessment should be acted upon to ensure this. It should be
demonstrated that the cost involved in reducing the risk further would
be disproportionate to the benefit gained. Determining that a risk has
been reduced to ALARP involves a comparison of the risk to be avoided
and an assessment of the measures required to achieve this in terms of
money, time, and effort (Cost Benefit Analysis). Conducting Risk
Assessments (RAs) is a legal requirement and also plays a vital role in:

-   Reducing accidents and incidents

-   Assisting in the decision-making process

-   Improving the understanding of safety around the organisation

-   Mitigate financial, legal, environmental, and reputational harm to
    the organisation

-   Enabling corporate resources to be directed where they are most
    needed

A RA shall only be conducted by people with sufficient knowledge and
understanding of the subject matter being evaluated.

The framework for managing risk applies to all functional areas at DWA.

##  Objectives 

The objective of a RA is to:

-   Identify and hazards and potential risk

-   Analyse risk and determine mitigating measures to improve safety,
    reduce impact to the environment and enhance business efficiency

-   Ensure that identified risks are reduced to a level that is both
    ALARP and Tolerable to the organisation.

## Scope 

RAs are applicable to all areas of DWA and should be completed and/or
reviewed:

-   Prior to the introduction of new or changes to existing procedures,
    processes, or equipment

-   Prior to the introduction of new or changes to existing tasks which
    have or may have potential health, safety, business, or
    environmental implications

-   Following an accident, incident, or near-miss

-   Following legislative or regulatory changes

-   As part of any project or system safety case

-   When undertaking workplace assessments

-   When completing safety surveys

-   At least on an annual basis (reviewed)

Confidence in safety is required before any changes to a system are put
into service; the risk assessment and mitigation process should
therefore start early in the lifecycle of a new system. For a large and
complex project, there will be several phases of Risk Assessment and
mitigation, each becoming more detailed as the design and development of
the system progresses. The final pre-implementation Risk Assessment then
forms the basis for the periodic safety reviews of the operational
system, which should continue throughout its lifecycle until
decommissioning.

## Definitions

**'Functional System'** means a combination of procedures, human
resources, and equipment, including hardware and software, organised to
perform a function within the context of ATM/ANS and other ATM network
functions, ( EU Reg 2017/373);

**'Non-Functional System'** means a change to the provision of service,
the service provider\'s management system and/or safety management
system, which does not affect the functional system.

**'Hazard'** means any condition, event, or circumstance which could
induce a harmful effect (EU Reg 2017/373);

**'Risk'** means the combination of the overall probability or frequency
of occurrence of a harmful effect induced by a hazard and the severity
of that effect (EU Reg 2017/373);

# Risk Assessment Process

Before starting the Risk Assessment (RA) process, in accordance **with
DWA SAF 01 Supp5 -- Change Management Procedures**, a change must be
assessed as either affecting the Functional or Non-Functional System,
and the appropriate assessment criteria must be followed (see sections 3
and 4).

Risk assessment and mitigation requires a systematic approach, and DWA
shall follow the Seven Step Approach to Risk Assessment (see Figure 1):

-   Step 1 - System description

-   Step 2 - Hazard and consequence identification

-   Step 3 - Estimation of the severity of the consequences of the
    hazard occurring

-   Step 4 - Estimation/assessment of the likelihood of the hazard
    consequences occurring

-   Step 5 - Evaluation of the risk

-   Step 6 - Risk mitigation and safety requirements

-   Step 7 - Claims, arguments, and evidence that the safety
    requirements have been met and documenting this in a safety case

Risk Assessments shall be carried out by a group of Suitably Qualified
and Experienced Personnel (SQEP). Where possible this same group of
Subject Matter Experts (SMEs) should be used throughout the entire
process to allow continuity.

The hazard identification process, carried out by suitably qualified
personnel, targets complete coverage of any condition, event, or
circumstance related to a change, which could induce a harmful effect.
Initially, all hazards are assessed without risk mitigations in order to
identify the harmful effects related to the identified hazards.

Hazards are identified through reactive proactive and predictive methods
of safety data collection such as;

-   Safety reporting

-   Internal investigation and trend analysis

-   Results from internal and external audits

-   Monitoring of day-to-day normal operations and environment

-   Results of accidents and serious incidents

-   Foreseeable events

#### Hazard Identification

**Credible Hazardous Event Description**[^1]. Having fully described the
system and its operational environment in **Step 1**, the next activity
is to describe all credible hazardous events that may arise in relation
to the situation, system, or the proposed change. These would normally
be high-level descriptions.

Descriptions should have a reasonable level of detail to allow a full
scenario description to be developed.

It should be noted that each Credible Hazardous Event is a **[top-level
event]{.underline}**; care should be taken not to confuse this with the
Cause (Trigger/threat) that initiates the event, nor the Consequence
(outcome) that happens as a result of the event.

**Accident Types (Section k)**

Accident types shall be listed in this section, the definition of an
accident according to Regulation (EU) 996/2010; Art 2 is:

'accident' means an occurrence associated with the operation of an
aircraft which, in the case of a manned aircraft, takes place between
the time any person boards the aircraft with the intention of flight
until such time as all such persons have disembarked, or in the case of
an unmanned aircraft, takes place between the time the aircraft is ready
to move with the purpose of flight until such time it comes to rest at
the end of the flight and the primary propulsion system is shut down, in
which:

\(a\) a person is fatally or seriously injured as a result of:

--- being in the aircraft, or,

--- direct contact with any part of the aircraft, including parts which
have become detached from the aircraft, or,

--- direct exposure to jet blast,

except when the injuries are from natural causes, self-inflicted or
inflicted by other persons, or when the injuries are to stowaways hiding
outside the areas normally available to the passengers and crew; or

\(b\) the aircraft sustains damage or structural failure which adversely
affects the structural strength, performance or flight characteristics
of the aircraft, and would normally require major repair or replacement
of the affected component, except for engine failure or damage, when the
damage is limited to a single engine, (including its cowlings or
accessories), to propellers, wing tips, antennas, probes, vanes, tires,
brakes, wheels, fairings, panels, landing gear doors, windscreens, the
aircraft skin (such as small dents or puncture holes) or minor damages
to main rotor blades, tail rotor blades, landing gear, and those
resulting from hail or bird strike, (including holes in the radome); or

\(c\) the aircraft is missing or is completely inaccessible

The list below is possible accidents, for example, as a (non-exhaustive)
guide they may include:

-   Controlled flight into terrain (CFIT) - an airworthy aircraft under
    the complete control of the pilot is inadvertently flown into
    terrain, water, or an obstacle

-   Mid Air Collision (MAC) - A mid-air collision is an accident in
    which two or more aircraft come into contact with each other while
    flying.

-   Ground Collision between two Aircraft or Aircraft and Ground
    Vehicle, Ground Collision Incident (GCI) - A ground collision is an
    accident that occurs on the ground, involving either two aircraft or
    an aircraft and a ground vehicle.

-   Aircraft Collision with Obstacles During Take-off or Landing
    (CTOL) - refers to an accident where an aircraft collides with
    obstacles such as buildings, trees, vehicles, or other objects
    during the critical phases of take-off or landing.

-   Runway Excursion (RE) - A runway excursion is an incident where an
    aircraft veers off or overruns the runway surface during take-off or
    landing.

-   Abnormal Runway Contact (ARC) - refers to any unintended or
    irregular interaction between an aircraft and the runway surface
    during landing or take-off. This can include:

    -   Hard Landing: When an aircraft impacts the runway with more
        force than usual.

    -   Tail Strike: When the tail of the aircraft contacts the runway
        during take-off or landing.

    -   Nose Gear Strike: When the nose gear impacts the runway more
        forcefully than intended.

    -   Bounce or Rebound: When the aircraft bounces upon landing and
        then makes subsequent contacts with the runway.

Potential hazards found to have no accident outcome shall be recorded
and 'nil' inserted in the accident type (section k.)

**Harmful Effect (Section l.)**

Record all harmful effects in section l. below is a list of possible
harmful effect, for example, as a (non-exhaustive) guide they may
include:

**Fatal injury** means an injury which is sustained by a person in an
accident, and which results in his or her death within 30 days of the
date of the accident.

**Serious injury** means an injury which is sustained by a person in an
accident and which involves one of the following:

a.  hospitalisation for more than 48 hours, commencing within 7 days
    > from the date the injury was received.

b.  a fracture of any bone (except simple fractures of fingers, toes, or
    > nose).

c.  lacerations which cause severe haemorrhage, nerve, muscle or tendon
    > damage.

d.  injury to any internal organ.

e.  second- or third-degree burns, or any burns affecting more than 5 %
    > of the body surface.

f.  verified exposure to infectious substances or harmful radiation.

**Aircraft Destroyed** - an aircraft is considered destroyed when it is
damaged to the extent that it cannot be repaired or restored to
airworthy condition. This includes total structural failure, extensive
fire damage, or complete disintegration.

**Aircraft Damaged** - refers to any physical harm to the aircraft that
affects its structural integrity, performance, or safety, but does not
render it completely destroyed. This can include damage to the fuselage,
wings, landing gear, or engines, minor structural damage, or cosmetic
damage that requires repair.

**Hazardous Event Cause Description (Threat/Trigger) (Section g.)**

Causes/Threats/Triggers are sources of harm that could lead to the top
event. They could be the result of a failure, an external influence, or
an operational issue. They can include system malfunctions, human
errors, or external factors that increase the likelihood of the top
event occurring. Any one Cause/Threat/Trigger must have the power to
cause the hazardous event on its own.

All credible Causes/Threats /Triggers for each Hazardous Event should be
listed.

**Hazard Identification And Determination Of Safety Criteria Form
note**: Each hazard cause should be noted in Section g.

**Hazardous Event Consequence Description (Harmful Effect) (Section
h.)**

Consequences represent the undesirable outcomes that could result from
the listed Hazardous events. These can range from environmental damage
to injuries, damaged reputation, or even loss of life. Identifying and
understanding potential consequences is crucial for effective risk
management.

For example, as a (non-exhaustive) guide they may include:

-   Loss of control (LOC) - aircraft enters a flight regime which is
    outside its normal envelope

-   Runway excursion (REX)- aircraft departs the runway in use during
    the take-off or landing run - the excursion may be intentional or
    unintentional

-   Runway incursion (RIN) - Any occurrence at an aerodrome involving
    the incorrect presence of an aircraft vehicle or person on the
    protected area of a surface designated for the landing and take-off
    of aircraft"

-   Loss of Separation (LOS) - specified separation minima are breached.

-   Undesired Ground Handling (GHE) Event

-   Other (OTH)

For trend analysis purposes, if possible, each Credible Hazardous Event
should be categorised against one of the above event types.

It should be noted that all credible hazards identified at DWA shall be
initially assigned the highest severity in the severity classification
scheme (severity 1), i.e. to reflect the assessment without mitigations
in place.

extensive experience in their respective fields, and the adoption of
standard likelihood classification schemes, should ensure that the
outcome will be an informed judgement. When considering the likelihood,
previous knowledge and existing information should be taken into
account. Consideration should also be given to the duration and
frequency of the activity (exposure).

Later on, after this 7-step procedure has been run through more than
once, evidence will start to be amassed that can be used to improve the
credibility of the likelihood assessment. This evidence will be needed
later in step 7 to help build the argument that the Safety Objectives
(i.e. the objective to ensure that all hazards are tolerable) have been
met.

Once the assessment of likelihood has been completed for all the
identified Hazards, the results, including the rationale for the
classification chosen, should be recorded in the Risk Assessment &
Evaluation Form.

**Risk Assessment & Evaluation Form Completion note**: The current
mitigated Likelihood assessment should be recorded in the Section p.
"Probability" section.

#### Risk Mitigation

The key activities in **Step 6** are:

a.  Mitigate the risks to levels that are Tolerable and ALARP; and

o.  Generate Safety Requirements.

Once **Step 5** has been, an attempt shall always be made to reduce the
risk to a level that is both Tolerable (within the Acceptable category)
and ALARP by using further mitigation measures. It is recommended to
apply the ALARP principle to all identified risks, even where the risks
already fall in the \'Acceptable\' region. This is in order to support
the goal of constant safety improvement whenever practicable.

The SMEs conducting the Risk Assessment shall strive to identify
additional mitigation measures (above and beyond those already
considered on current mitigated risk classification) that can be
implanted to further reduce the risk. The identification of a new risk
mitigation measure requires a good understanding of why the hazard is
likely to manifest, and the factors contributing to the severity of its
consequences, since any mechanism that will be effective in reducing
risk will have to modify one or more of these factors.

Risk mitigation measures may work through reducing the probability of
occurrence, or the severity of the consequences, or both. Achieving the
desired level of risk reduction may require the implementation of more
than one mitigation measure.

The effectiveness of any proposed risk mitigation measure must be
assessed by first examining closely whether the implementation of the
mitigation measures might introduce any new hazards or whether they
change the basis on which other assessments have been made. Having
decided that a simple mitigation measure may be suitable, it will often
be necessary to repeat **Steps 3, 4** and **5** in order to evaluate the
acceptability of the risk with that proposed new mitigation measure in
place.

Once the new (proposed) mitigation measures have been identified and
described (Risk Assessment & Evaluation Form section p.) the Severity
and Likelihood should be recalculated (Risk Assessment & Evaluation Form
section q to give a Final Risk Classification.

## Safety Criteria

Dublin Weston Airport shall determine the safety acceptability of a
change to a functional system, based on the analysis of the risks posed
by the introduction of the change, differentiated on basis of types of
operations and stakeholder classes, as appropriate.[^4]

The safety acceptability of a change shall be assessed by using specific
and verifiable safety criteria, where each criterion is expressed in
terms of an explicit, quantitative level of safety risk.[^5] The safety
criteria should be expressed as per the risk tolerability table, **Table
7** for changes to a functional system or **Table 12** for changes to a
non-functional system.

The safety criteria shall be determined by and be specific to the type
of change being implemented. They shall support the risk analysis in
terms of safety risk before, during and after the implementation of the
change and shall be documented using Table 2 Template -- Hazard
Identification Form.

To ensure that the functional system being changed remains as safe as it
was before the change, DWA shall use previous safety cases as an aid for
the determination of safety criteria, thus ensuring that the safety
criteria for the specified change can be maintained at an acceptable
level.

If the change is new to the DWA functional system and reference to
previous safety cases cannot be made, the safety criteria for the change
may be set by utilising the DWA risk register in order to reference
already identified risk at DWA and ensuring that the functional system
shall remain as safe as it was before the change.

DWA shall ensure that the safety criteria:[^6]

(1) are justified for the specific change, taking into account the type
    of change;

(2) when fulfilled, predict that the functional system after the change
    will be as safe as it was before the change or the air traffic
    services provider shall provide an argument justifying that:

    i.  any temporary reduction in safety will be offset by future
        > improvement in safety; or

    ii. any permanent reduction in safety has other beneficial
        > consequences;

(3) when taken collectively, ensure that the change does not create an
    unacceptable risk to the safety of the service;

(4) support the improvement of safety whenever reasonably practicable.

The Senior Management levels that have authority to make decisions
regarding the classification of safety criteria are:

-   Accountable Manager

-   ATM/ANS/ Safety Manager

-   Airfield Operations Manager

## Point to Note:

Several different hazards may all lead to the same consequence
(accident/incident).

Where this is the case, it is not sufficient to assess the tolerability
of each hazard independently because this may be misleading.

For example, there may be fifty hazards that all lead to the same
undesirable consequence, where each hazard has a very low probability of
occurring e.g. \'extremely remote\'. When considering the tolerability
of each individual hazard and consequence, it may be found that each one
sits in the \'ACCEPTABLE\' region of the Tolerability Matrix.

However, when all fifty hazards are considered together there will be an
increase in the probability of the consequence occurring that may move
the consequence from the \'ACCEPTABLE\' region to the \'UNDESIRABLE\' or
\'UNACCEPTABLE\' region of the Table.

It is therefore important to identify those hazards that contribute to
the same consequence and add the probabilities together to get an
overall probability of the consequence occurring. It is this overall
probability for the consequence occurring that is used to identify where
the consequence sits in Table 3 above.

The Safety Objective from these risk assessment activities is to ensure
that all risks associated with any hazardous activities at DWA fall
within the \'Acceptable\' cells of the matrix.

# SAFETY ASSESSMENT AND ASSURANCE OF CHANGES TO THE DWA FUNCTIONAL SYSTEM

The functional system shall be defined at DWA as:

"Any System, Equipment item, Infrastructure item, Physical Environment,
Process, Procedure or Staff Member that [directly]{.underline} plays a
role in the operational functioning of the Air Traffic Management (ATM)
or Airfield Operations at Dublin Weston Airport."

As per EU regulation 373/2017 -- ATS.OR.205, any change notified in
accordance with point ATM/ANS.OR.A.045(a)(1), DWA shall:

\(1\) ensure that a safety assessment is carried out covering the scope
of the change, which is:

\(i\) the equipment, procedural and human elements being changed;

\(ii\) interfaces and interactions between the elements being changed
and the remainder of the functional system;

\(iii\) interfaces and interactions between the elements being changed
and the context in which it is intended to operate;

\(iv\) the life cycle of the change from definition to operations
including transition into service;

\(v\) planned degraded modes of operation of the functional system; and

\(2\) provide assurance, with sufficient confidence, via a complete,
documented and valid argument that the safety criteria identified via
the application of point ATS.OR.210 are valid, will be satisfied and
will remain satisfied.

\(b\) An air traffic services provider shall ensure that the safety
assessment referred to in point (a) comprises:

\(1\) the identification of hazards;

\(2\) the determination and justification of the safety criteria
applicable to the change in accordance with point ATS.OR.210;

\(3\) the risk analysis of the effects related to the change;

\(4\) the risk evaluation and, if required, risk mitigation for the
change such that it can meet the applicable safety criteria;

\(5\) the verification that:

\(i\) the assessment corresponds to the scope of the change as defined
in point (a)(1);

\(ii\) the change meets the safety criteria;

\(6\) the specification of the monitoring criteria necessary to
demonstrate that the service delivered by the changed functional system
will continue to meet the safety criteria.

Examples of functional system items could be (but are not limited to), a
radio system or information system used by an Air Traffic Control
Officer (ATCO), the runways or taxiways, airfield fire-fighting
equipment, aircraft towing or refuelling procedures , ground handling
staff, etc.

Therefore any change likely to affect the current operations of any of
those elements could be considered a change to the functional system.
